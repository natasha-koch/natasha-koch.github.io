Context

Eroma had over 30,000 active customers spanning individual makers and hobbyists through to professional candle brands supplying some of Australia's leading retailers. Their digital platform was the backbone of the business, 95% of orders were placed online. When Eroma commissioned a greenfields platform, I led the end-to-end UX and product design across a three-year engagement.

The challenge

Dual Audience

One platform for two fundamentally different customers. Retail consumers browsing and discovering. B2B buyers placing large repeat orders with tiered pricing, account requirements, and specific fulfilment needs. Every UX decision had to work for both without compromising either.

Vast Product Catalogue

The catalogue spanned candle jars, waxes, wicks, fragrances, lids, packaging, and accessories, many with multiple configurations, colour variants, and size options. Products frequently needed to be understood in relation to each other, requiring careful information architecture and considered cross-sell thinking throughout.

Multi-Warehouse Fulfilment

Eroma operated multiple warehouse and showroom locations across Australia, with stock levels and availability varying by location. A single order could involve stock shipped from more than one warehouse, each with different delivery costs, or a user might choose to pick up instead. This required a level of inventory transparency and user control that standard ecommerce platforms were not designed to handle.

My approach

The engagement began with discovery, working closely with the client to understand the product catalogue, customer types, and the operational complexity of the business. From there I followed a deliberate sequencing approach: establish the core product entity first, then build outward. The Product Display Page (PDP) was the starting point, working through to the Product Listing Page (PLP), mini cart, checkout, and account areas, ensuring UX patterns were established at the foundation before the wider site was built on top of them.

Wireframes were produced first across all major flows and handed to development to begin building, allowing the front-end team to establish functionality while UI design continued in parallel. This agile approach kept the project moving without requiring fully polished designs before development could start.

Throughout the engagement I built and maintained a comprehensive design system that scaled across two years and multiple developers without losing coherence. Built from the ground up as a component library and UI pattern framework, with accessibility embedded as a foundational principle, it established visual consistency across the site while providing the development team a reliable, reusable foundation to build from.

Agile: wireframes first, development in parallel

UX & product design

Transparency & Fulfilment

Transparency was the guiding UX principle, a reflection of Eroma's relationship with their customer base. The mini cart had to handle delivery versus pickup, show which warehouses items were coming from, display delivery costs per warehouse split, and let users toggle between fulfilment options to compare. Stock visibility was surfaced at every touchpoint: PDP, mini cart, checkout, so users were never surprised. I designed the out-of-stock and reduced-stock flows to notify users early and clearly.

Product Complexity

The PDP was built on a modular component system, configurable to each product's specific needs. A user could order a pack of forty-eight, two packs of twelve, and individual items, all in one add-to-cart action. The system had to make this scannable and intuitive, not overwhelming, across both simple single-purchase products and the most complex configurable lines.

Dual-Audience Navigation

The standard PLP served the broader catalogue, but fragrances required a fully custom PLP. It needed to serve users who knew exactly what they wanted by name, those browsing by scent type or season, and those browsing without a specific fragrance in mind. I designed a triple navigation system on a single page: search, category filtering, and A-Z navigation. B2B customers needing to bypass the catalogue entirely were served by Quick Order for efficient repeat procurement.

Checkout & Account

Before a single checkout screen was designed, I mapped the complete user flows covering every user type, decision point, and edge case. The account area went beyond Adobe Commerce's native capabilities, with wishlists serving both audiences, functioning as familiar lists for B2C customers and as named requisition lists for efficient B2B repeat ordering.

How I worked

I maintained an ongoing collaborative client relationship while simultaneously managing multiple complex and interdependent workstreams. To manage this at scale, I developed systems for each workstream.

Design-to-development handovers were timed to when a developer was ready to pick up the work, not when designs were finished, avoiding context being lost between handover and implementation. Developers were asked to review designs beforehand so sessions could focus on questions and decisions rather than first impressions.

Design QA was managed through a structured Jira-based system, broken down by feature or section and documented in a way that any developer could pick up a ticket regardless of whether they had been on the original work. A traffic light system tracked status across iterations keeping quality assurance moving.

I established a design-to-test process that kept testers focused on functional validation while maintaining design intent. One tester told me at the end of the project she'd learned more about UX and design thinking from working on Eroma than she'd expected.

Working closely with front-end developers, I actively lifted design literacy across the team, helping developers approach problems through patterns and reusable systems rather than one-off solutions, a perspective informed equally by my background in both design and front-end development.

Outcome

The engagement exceeded 1,000 hours of design work across three years. Aligent later selected it as a flagship case study, using it in client proposals and featuring it in an 11-minute process video documenting the design approach.
